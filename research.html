Overview

Advances in sensitivity and resolution from cutting edge radio observatories such as the GBT, ALMA, and the JVLA are enabling the study of the earliest, coldest, and most deeply embedded phases of high-mass star formation. I am interested in determining the physical and chemical properties of high-mass star forming molecular cloud "clumps" in different evolutionary stages. A goal of this research is to also systematically uncover a population of high-mass pre-protoclusters or "starless clumps."

Water Masers in High-Mass Star Forming Regions

The GBT BGPS ammonia and water maser has observed over 1500 clumps in a variety of evolutionary stages. We calculate mean gas kinetic temperatures from simultaneous observataion of the (1,1), (2,2), and (3,3) inversion transitions of ammonia. The water maser provides a unique sign-post of star formation in classifying clumps by evolutionary stage. We find that for existing publicly available catalogs of color-selected YSO's (Lumsden et al. 2013, Robitaille et al. 2008), 25% host water masers but do not have cataloged IR activity.

The Physical Properties of Clumps in Different Evolutionary Stages

The evolutinoary sequence of high-mass star forming systems is more poorly understood than low-mass systems because they are far away, deeply embedded, environmentally complex, and have until very recently not had blind, systematic surveys from which to measure their properties. Using the BGPS and a wide variety of Galactic plane surveys, we categorize clumps into evolutionary stages from a diverse set of sign-posts of star formation activity, including water masers, methanol masers, color-selected YSOs, UCHII regions, and HiGal 70 micron point sources.
