Overview

Advances in sensitivity and resolution from cutting edge radio observatories such as the GBT, JVLA, and ALMA are enabling the study of the earliest, coldest, and most deeply embedded phases of high-mass star formation. I am interested in studying the properties of the molecular cloud "clumps" that precursors to stellar clusters. Because high-mass stars uniquely form in clusters, these regions represent the initial conditions of high-mass star formation. What are their physical and chemical properties? How do gas flows feed the growth of high-mass stars? Do high-mass (>40 solar masses) starless cores exist? How do clumps fragment into a population of cores?

Water Masers in High-Mass Star Forming Regions

In the GBT BGPS ammonia and water maser survey we present targeted observations toward over 1780 clumps in a variety of evolutionary stages. We calculate mean gas kinetic temperatures from simultaneous observataion of the (1,1), (2,2), and (3,3) inversion transitions of ammonia. These temperatures provide essential information in calculating accurate masses from dust continuum emission. We also provide a catalog of 472 22 GHz water maser spectra, 343 of which have no counterpart in the published surveys from the Mopra and Arcetri observatories. We find that the water maser provides a unique sign-post of star formation activity when compared with existing catalogs of color-selected YSO's (Lumsden et al. 2013, Robitaille et al. 2008): 25% of clumps that host water masers do not have cataloged IR activity. This work was included in §2 and §3 in Svoboda et al. (2016) .

Physical Properties of Massive Starless and Star-forming Clumps

The evolutinoary sequence of high-mass star forming systems is more poorly understood than low-mass systems because they are far away, deeply embedded, environmentally complex, and have until very recently not had blind, systematic surveys from which to measure their properties. Using the BGPS and a wide variety of Galactic plane surveys, we categorize clumps into evolutionary stages from a diverse set of sign-posts of star formation activity, including water masers, methanol masers, color-selected YSOs, UCHII regions, and HiGal 70 micron point sources. Svoboda et al. (2016) .
