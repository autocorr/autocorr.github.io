Overview

Advances in sensitivity and resolution from cutting edge radio observatories such as the GBT, ALMA, and the JVLA are enabling the study of the earliest, coldest, and most deeply embedded phases of high-mass star formation. I am interested in determining the physical and chemical properties of high-mass star forming molecular cloud "clumps" in different evolutionary stages. A goal of this research is to also systematically uncover a population of high-mass pre-protoclusters or "starless clumps."

Origins of the most Massive Stars

The most massive stars are the primary actors in galaxy evolution and drastically affect the environments that form planetary and stellar systems. High-mass stars drive the heating and turbulence in the interstellar medium as well enrich the interstellar medium with heavy elements that are the dominant coolant. Despite the importance of high-mass stars, their formation is poorly understood due to observational difficulty. Compared to low-mass, high-mass star forming regions are more distant, are deeply embedded in their natal clouds, and have shorter time-scales for critical evolutionary phases. However, we live in an exciting age where, for the first time, sensitive surveys of our Galaxy are taking place in a variety of wavelengths and observational probes of star formation. My research focuses on the recently publicly released Bolocam Galactic Plane Survey (BGPS). The BGPS used the Caltech Submillimeter Observatory (CSO) to observe 1.1mm radio continuum of cool dust in molecular cloud "clumps." The BGPS reveals the most complete census to date of high-mass star forming regions in all stages of evolution throughout our Galaxy.

Starless Clumps in the BGPS

My research aims to determine the physical properties (temperature, mass, size, etc.) of high-mass star forming regions from the BGPS to determine how these properties change based on the region's evolutionary stage. In the search for the earliest phase of high-mass star formation, the so-called "pre-protoclusters" or "starless clumps," I have compared the regions in the BGPS to a variety of observations known to be "sign-posts" of star formation activity. These sign-posts range from infrared observations of young protostars, water and methanol masers, molecular outflows, and ultra-compact HII regions. Water masers occur in the outflows of young, newly forming stars and their presence strongly suggests that the region as a whole is undergoing active star formation. I have reduced and analyzed a sample of ~1400 water maser spectra from the 100m Green Bank Telescope, the largest such set of such observations taken from a blind survey. Since starless clumps are not expected to host water masers, these data provide a sensitive sign-post of star-formation activity in addition to those found in publicly available surveys.
